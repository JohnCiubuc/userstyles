    General Information

    Classifications

    • traditional classification of pulmonary edema(1)
      • cardiogenic - result of increased pulmonary capillary hydrostatic pressure (often in context of left heart disease)
      • noncardiogenic - occurs due to increased pulmonary capillary endothelial permeability following direct or indirect lung injury
    • significant overlap is possible, with patients presenting features of both cardiogenic and noncardiogenic edema (such as in transfusion-related acute lung injury, high altitude pulmonary edema or neurogenic pulmonary edema

    Differential Diagnosis

    Causes by edema type

    Causes of cardiogenic edema

    • causes of cardiogenic edema include
      • mitral stenosis
      • mitral regurgitation
      • aortic stenosis
      • aortic regurgitation
      • heart failure with reduced ejection fraction, which can be seen in patients with
        • ischemic cardiomyopathy
        • tachyarrhythmia, such as in atrial fibrillation
        • dilated cardiomyopathy
        • peripartum cardiomyopathy
        • takotsubo syndrome
      • restrictive cardiomyopathy
      • heart failure with preserved ejection fraction
      • hypertrophic cardiomyopathy
      • hyperperfusion with blood, blood products, or fluids, as seen in(1, 2)
        • transfusion-associated circulatory overload (TACO)
        • volume overload
        • patients receiving chronic dialysis (Nat Rev Dis Primers 2017 Nov 23;3():17088)
      • high dietary salt load(4)
      • uncontrolled hypertension(4)
      • myocardial infarction(4)
      • pheochromocytoma(4)
      • renal artery stenosis(4)

    Causes of noncardiogenic edema

    • causes of noncardiogenic edema include
      • acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS), such as that seen in transfusion-related acute lung injury (TRALI)
      • negative-pressure pulmonary edema due to upper airway obstruction, as can be seen in(3, 4)
        • postextubation laryngospasm
        • foreign body aspiration
        • inspissated tracheal secretions
        • hiccups
        • thyroid goiter
        • temporomandibular joint arthroscopy
        • difficult intubation
        • hematoma
        • upper airway tumor
        • oropharyngeal surgery
        • Ludwig angina
        • acromegaly
        • mediastinal tumor
        • severe patient-ventilator asynchrony
        • sleep-disordered breathing
      • ascent to elevation > 2,500 meters (8,202 feet) (high altitude pulmonary edema)
      • central nervous system injury (neurogenic pulmonary edema)
      • ischemic/reperfusion injury after lung transplant(1)
      • rapid removal of air or fluid from pleural cavity, such as during thoracentesis (re-expansion edema)(1)
      • capillary leak syndrome, causes of which include
        • idiopathic systemic capillary leak syndrome (Clarkson disease)
        • medications, such as interleukin-2, gemcitabine, or monoclonal antibodies
        • engraftment syndrome after hematopoietic stem cell transplant
        • retinoic acid (differentiation syndrome)
        • ovarian hyperstimulation syndrome
        • hemophagocytic lymphohistiocytosis
        • viral hemorrhagic fever
        • autoimmune diseases
        • snakebite envenomation
        • ricin poisoning
        • Reference - Kidney Int 2017 Jul;92(1):37

    Pathogenesis

    Normal fluid exchange

    • discussion of mechanisms of pulmonary edema formation may be framed in context of Starling's equation, which describes transendothelial fluid exchange in pulmonary capillary endothelium(1, 3)
      Qf = Kf ([Pc - Pi ] - σ[πc - πi ])
      • Qf is net fluid movement from capillary lumen to alveolar interstitium
      • [Pc - Pi ] is hydrostatic pressure where c is capillary and i is interstitial
      • σ is reflection coefficient indicating endothelial permeability to proteins
      • [πc - πi ] is oncotic pressure where c is capillary and i is interstitial
      • Kf is coefficient of capillary permeability (includes hydraulic conductivity and filtration surface area)
    • in normal lung
      • permeability of endothelium to both water and protein determines volume of fluid that forms at any given net force(1)
      • hydrostatic pressure favors net fluid flow from capillaries to interstitium, with subsequent fluid drainage through pulmonary lymphatics(1, 3)
      • oncotic pressure(2)
        • endothelial glycocalyx (network of glycosaminoglycans and proteins lining pulmonary capillary endothelium) collects serum plasma proteins, forming ultrafiltrate layer between endothelium and glycocalyx
        • ultrafiltrate layer generates oncotic force, pulling fluid into intravascular compartment
    • formula for estimating pulmonary capillary hydrostatic pressure using left atrial pressure(2)
      Pmv = PLa + 0.4(MPAP-PLa)
      • Pmv is pulmonary capillary hydrostatic pressure
      • PLa is left atrial pressure
      • MPAP is mean pulmonary pressure

    Mechanisms of edema formation

    • general causes of edema formation
      • in hydrostatic edema(2, 4)
        • left atrial and pulmonary capillary pressure rises following left ventricular pressure increase (such as in left ventricle failure) or volume overload
        • pressure increase causes increased wall tension in capillaries leading to increased fluid flow into interstitium, eventually surpassing drainage capacity of pulmonary lymphatics
        • extravascular lung water accumulates, followed by formation of alveolar edema
        • hydrostatic pressure is opposed by colloid osmotic pressure in capillaries (primarily driven by albumin)
      • in permeability edema, damage to ultrafiltrate layer created by endothelial glycocalyx that lines pulmonary capillary endothelium (such as in surgical trauma or ischemic/reperfusion injury) increases capillary permeability, leading to increased extravasation of protein-rich fluid and subsequent edema(2)
    • stages of pulmonary fluid accumulation and edema development(2)
      • stage 1 (compensated) - increased fluid accumulation with parallel increase in lymphatic flow; no net fluid accumulation
      • stage 2 (perihilar edema) - fluid accumulation overwhelms lymphatic drainage and edema develops around bronchioles and lung vasculature
      • stage 3 (alveolar edema)
        • interstitial fluid continues to accumulate around periphery of alveolar membrane
        • over time fluid crosses alveolar wall, leading to alveolar flooding

    Mechanisms of edema resolution

    • vectorial ion transplant is primary driver of pulmonary edema resolution
      • osmotic gradient for reabsorption of water from alveoli into interstitium is created
        • sodium ions taken up by apical sodium channels on alveolar epithelial type 1 and 2 cells, with subsequent passage into lung interstitium via sodium-potassium ATPase(3)
        • chloride ion transport mediated by cystic fibrosis transmembrane conductance regulator (CFTR), which is expressed on apical membranes of distal airway and alveolar epithelial cells ( Front Immunol 2017;8:393 full-text)
      • water passively follows ions through aquaporins (water channels found mainly on alveolar epithelial type I cells)(5)
      • fluid moved to interstitium subsequently drained via pulmonary lymphatics(3)

    General Evaluation

    Clinical presentation

    • clinical presentation is variable, and features of cardiogenic and noncardiogenic edema can overlap(1)
    • acute pulmonary edema usually presents as medical emergency, and symptoms can include
      • severe, rapidly-progressive dyspnea(1, 4)
      • tachypnea(1, 2)
      • orthopnea(2)
      • pink frothy sputum (in endotracheal tube if intubated; or as expectoration)(3, 4)
    • patients may also present with sympathetic nervous system activation that can manifest as(4)
      • tachycardia
      • cold skin
      • pallor
      • sweating
    • patients often present with acute symptoms in middle of night(4)
    • physical examination and laboratory testing may show
      • hypoxemia(1, 4)
      • hypertension(4)
      • crackles (rales) on lung auscultation (typically best heard in posterior basilar regions in supine patients)(2)

    Diagnostic approach

    Initial evaluation

    • in patients with acute pulmonary edema, initial assessment to determine cardiogenic or noncardiogenic cause should include clinical history, physical examination, laboratory testing, and chest x-ray
      • history, physical exam, and laboratory testing findings
        • for cardiogenic edema
          • history of
            • valvular disease(1)
            • tachyarrhythmia(4)
            • myocardial infarction or congestive heart failure(5)
            • recent transfusion with blood, blood products, or fluids(1, 2)
            • uncontrolled hypertension or high salt intake(4)
          • low cardiac output state(5)
          • third heart sound on auscultation(5)
          • peripheral edema(5)
          • jugular venous distension(5)
          • elevated cardiac enzymes (such as troponin)(5)
          • elevated brain natriuretic peptide levels(5)
        • for noncardiogenic edema
          • history of
            • infection (pulmonary or nonpulmonary)(5)
            • aspiration or other cause of upper airway obstruction(5)
            • ascent to high elevation(2)
            • central nervous system injury(2)
            • recent lung transplant causing ischemic/reperfusion injury(2)
            • medication or therapy known to cause capillary leak syndrome ( Kidney Int 2017 Jul;92(1):37)
          • hyperdynamic state(5)
          • elevated white blood cell count(5)
          • laboratory findings suggestive of pancreatitis or peritonitis(5)
          • normal brain natriuretic peptide levels(5)
        • see Natriuretic peptide and biomarkers testing for heart failure and Cardiac troponin testing for additional information

    Additional evaluation

    Lung ultrasound

    • bedside lung ultrasound in emergency (BLUE) protocol can be used to diagnose acute respiratory failure or when physical examination inconclusive in patients with respiratory distress
      • signs seen in fluid accumulation
        • B-lines (forming lung rockets if ≥ 3 between 2 ribs) - presence rules out pneumothorax; lung rockets associated with interstitial syndrome (such as pulmonary edema)
          • always comet-tail artifact
          • always arises from pleural line
          • always moves simultaneously with lung sliding
          • typically long and extending to lower edge of screen
          • typically hyperechoic like pleural line
          • typically well-defined
          • typically obliterates A-lines
        • sinusoid sign - in free pleural effusions, visceral pleura lung line moves toward pleural line during inspiration, creating sinusoid sign; together with quad sign associated with pleural effusion
        • fractal (or shred) sign - pleural effusion with identifiable visceral pleura lung line with shredded deep border underneath; associated with nontranslobular consolidations
      • Typical Lung Ultrasound Features by Type of Edema:

        • Ground glass rockets (B lines)

        • Normal lung sliding
        • Homogenous lung tissue

        Noncardiogenic

    Transthoracic echocardiography (TTE)

    • TTE usually obtained in the evaluation of patients with pulmonary edema to determine underlying cause(5)
      • cardiogenic edema
        • suggested by presence of enlarged cardiac chambers and/or decreased left ventricular function, although normal findings do not rule out cardiogenic cause
        • may indicate
          • heart failure with reduced ejection fraction, as can be seen in patients with
            • ischemic cardiomyopathy
            • dilated cardiomyopathy
            • peripartum cardiomyopathy
            • takotsubo syndrome
          • restrictive cardiomyopathy
          • heart failure with preserved ejection fraction
          • hypertrophic cardiomyopathy
      • noncardiogenic edema suggested by normal or small cardiac chamber size and normal left ventricular function
      • DynaMed commentary -- Some patients with noncardiogenic pulmonary edema from acute respiratory distress syndrome and sepsis may have dilated cardiac chambers and left ventricular dysfunction.

    Electrocardiogram (ECG)

    • if suspected cardiogenic edema due to heart failure, consider ECG to identify potential cause
      • abnormal ECG has limited positive predictive value for heart failure, but completely normal ECG makes heart failure with systolic function unlikely (< 10%)
      • common ECG abnormalities in heart failure and potential causes
        • sinus tachycardia - decompensated heart failure, anemia, fever, hyperthyroidism
        • sinus bradycardia - beta blocker, digoxin, ivabradine, verapamil, diltiazem, anti-arrhythmic, hypothyroidism, sick sinus syndrome
        • atrial tachyarrhythmia - hyperthyroidism, infection, mitral valve disease, decompensated heart failure, infarction
        • ventricular arrhythmia - ischemia, infarction, cardiomyopathy, myocarditis, hypokalemia, hypomagnesemia, digitalis overdose
        • myocardial ischemia/infarction - coronary artery disease
        • atrioventricular block - infarction, drug toxicity, myocarditis, sarcoidosis, Lyme disease, genetic cardiomyopathy (such as laminopathy, desminopathy)
        • Q waves - infarction, hypertrophic cardiomyopathy, left bundle branch block, preexcitation
        • QRS > 120 milliseconds with left bundle branch block morphology - electromechanical dyssynchrony
        • left ventricular hypertrophy - hypertension, aortic valve disease, hypertrophic cardiomyopathy
        • microvoltage - obesity, emphysema, pericardial effusion, amyloidosis
      • ambulatory ECG monitoring may be useful if
        • symptoms suggestive of arrhythmia or bradycardia, such as palpitations or syncope
        • atrial fibrillation, to monitor ventricular rate
      • Reference - Eur Heart J 2012 Jul;33(14):1787
    • see Acute heart failure, Heart failure with reduced ejection fraction, and Heart failure with preserved ejection fraction for additional information

    Pulmonary artery catheterization

    • pulmonary artery catheterization not routinely used, but can be considered in patients with uncertain diagnosis after initial assessment and testing(5)
    • consider cardiogenic edema if pulmonary artery occlusion pressure > 18 mm Hg and noncardiogenic edema if ≤ 18 mm Hg(5)
    • catheterization allows for performance of transpulmonary thermodilution procedure to assess cardiac output(2)
      • involves injection of cold saline bolus into central venous circulation with subsequent measurement of change in blood temperature
      • establishes thermodilution curve that allows for calculation of hemodynamic variables (such as cardiac index or extravascular lung water index)
      • assumes homogenous distribution of pulmonary perfusion and accuracy may be dependent upon factors affecting ventilation/perfusion relationship, such as hypoxic pulmonary vasoconstriction, lung injury, vascular obstruction, positive end-expiratory pressure, spontaneous breathing, or lung resection
    • see Hemodynamic monitoring and Pulmonary artery catheter for additional information

    Management

    Acute management

    • primary goal in acute pulmonary edema is to reduce pulmonary fluid levels by shifting fluid from lung tissue to systemic circulation through any of the following(4)
      • reducing pulmonary capillary hydrostatic pressure (can be achieved using vasodilators)
      • increasing lymphatic drainage by reducing systemic venous pressure (can be achieved using vasodilators)
      • redistributing fluid from alveoli into lymphatics (can be partially achieved by ventilatory support with positive end expiratory pressure)
      • altering capillary permeability
    • therapies to consider for management of acute pulmonary edema(3, 4)
      • oxygenation and ventilatory support
        • oxygen is usually provided if patient is hypoxemic and no contraindications due to risk of carbon dioxide retention
        • positive end-expiratory pressure (PEEP) (using either noninvasive or invasive ventilation) to improve gas exchange, reduce work of breathing, and directly redistribute fluid from alveoli into pulmonary lymphatics for drainage
        • positive pressure ventilation to reduce venous return and wedge pressure
      • medications
        • intravenous loop diuretics (e.g. furosemide) given routinely to reduce circulating fluid volume
        • vasodilators (such as nitrate therapy or ACE inhibitors) to reduce left ventricular filling pressure
        • inotropic support if low cardiac output and/or blood pressure and patient resistant to initial therapy
      • conflicting recommendations on use of opiates, such as morphine, to relieve distress, anxiety, pain, and dyspnea(4)
        • morphine is venodilator and weak arterial vasodilator that suppresses feeling of air hunger ( CMAJ. 2007 Mar 13;176(6):797-805 full-text)
        • in patients with severe dyspnea, opiates may be considered to relieve dyspnea and anxiety but nausea and hypopnea may occur (ESC Class IIb, Level B) ( Eur Heart J. 2016 Jul 14;37(27):2129-200 full-text)
        • do not routinely offer opiates to patients with acute heart failure ( NICE Acute Heart failure: diagnosis and management 2014 Oct)
        • IV opiates may not be associated with reduced dyspnea in patients with cardiogenic pulmonary edema (level 2 [mid-level] evidence)
          • based on retrospective cohort study
          • 1,052 patients (51.4% of whom received IV opiates) with cardiogenic pulmonary edema included
          • opiates not associated with improvement in dyspnea as measured by visual analog scale compared to no opiates
          • mortality 10.4% with opiates vs. 8.8% with no opiates (not significant)
          • Reference - QJM 2010 Aug;103(8):573 full-text

    Management of common causes of pulmonary edema

    Acute cardiogenic pulmonary edema

    Acute heart failure

    • management of patients with acute heart failure
      • high-flow oxygen recommended in patients with capillary oxygen saturation < 90% or partial pressure of oxygen in arterial blood (PaO2) < 60 mm Hg (8 kilopascals [kPa]) (ESC Class I Level C)
      • fluid management
        • IV loop diuretics recommended if evidence of significant fluid overload (ACCF/AHA Class I, Level B) to improve breathlessness and relieve congestion (ESC Class I, Level C)
          • for example, furosemide (Lasix) 40-100 mg IV bolus, double dose in 30 minutes if no effect
          • increase dose or add second diuretic (for example, a thiazide) if inadequate to relieve congestion (ACCF/AHA Class IIa, Level B)
          • monitor symptoms, urine output, renal function, and electrolytes during use of IV diuretics (ESC Class I, Level C)
          • continuous infusion of loop diuretics appears no more effective than intermittent boluses (level 2 [mid-level] evidence)
          • see Diuretics for heart failure for complete information
        • conflicting recommendations on use of low-dose dopamine
          • European Society of Intensive Care Medicine (ESICM) recommends against use of low-dose dopamine for protection against acute kidney injury in the critically ill (ESICM Grade 1A) ( Intensive Care Med 2017 Jun;43(6):730 full-text)
          • American College of Cardiology recommends that low-dose dopamine infusion may be considered in addition to loop diuretic therapy for improvement of diuresis in patients with decompensated heart failure (ACCF/AHA Class IIb, Level B)
        • vasodilators (IV nitroglycerin, nitroprusside, or nesiritide) may be considered as adjunct to diuretics to relieve dyspnea in patients with acutely decompensated heart failure in absence of symptomatic hypotension (ACCF/AHA Class IIb, Level A)
          • nesiritide (Natrecor) may not reduce mortality, rehospitalization, or dyspnea in patients with acute decompensated heart failure (level 2 [mid-level] evidence) and increases risk for hypotension
          • nitrates for acute heart failure syndrome have insufficient evidence for effects on clinical outcomes
        • consider ultrafiltration for refractory congestion (ACCF/AHA Class IIa, Level C), but comparisons with diuresis have inconsistent results including increased risk for serious adverse events (level 2 [mid-level] evidence)
      • ventilatory support
        • consider noninvasive ventilation in patients with respiratory distress (respiratory rate > 25 breaths/minute, transcutaneous oxygen saturation < 90%) and start as early as possible to improve breathlessness and reduce rate of mechanical endotracheal intubation (ESC Class IIa, Level B)
          • use with caution in hypotensive patients as it may reduce blood pressure
          • monitor blood pressure regularly when treatment used
        • see Noninvasive positive pressure ventilation (NPPV) in adults for complete information
        • intubation recommended if unable to noninvasively manage respiratory failure leading to (ESC Class I, Level C)
          • hypoxemia (partial pressure of oxygen in arterial blood < 60 mm Hg [8 kilopascal])
          • hypercapnia (partial pressure of carbon dioxide in arterial blood > 50 mm Hg [6.65 kilopascal])
          • acidosis (pH < 7.35)
      • IV opiates (for example, morphine) may be considered in patients with severe dyspnea and anxiety, but nausea and hypopnea may occur (ESC Class IIb, Level B)
      • consider short-term, continuous IV inotropic support (such as dopamine, dobutamine, or milrinone) in hospitalized patients with documented severe systolic dysfunction, low blood pressure, and significantly depressed cardiac output (ACCF/AHA Class IIb, Level B)
      • monitor fluid intake and output, weight, serum electrolytes, blood urea nitrogen (BUN), and creatinine daily during use of IV diuretics or active medication titration (ACCF/AHA Class I, Level C)
      • invasive hemodynamic monitoring
        • recommended to guide therapy if respiratory distress or clinical evidence of impaired perfusion with inability to assess intracardiac filling pressures clinically (ACCF/AHA Class I, Level C)
        • pulmonary artery catheterization may increase adverse events (level 2 [mid-level] evidence) and does not reduce mortality or length of hospitalization (level 1 [likely reliable] evidence)
      • during symptomatic exacerbation of heart failure requiring hospitalization in patients on chronic maintenance treatment with guideline-directed medical therapy, continuation of guideline-directed medical therapy recommended in absence of hemodynamic instability or contraindications (ACCF/AHA Class I, Level B) ( Circulation 2013 Oct 15;128(16):e240)
    • see also
      • Acute heart failure
      • Cardiogenic shock
      • Acute coronary syndromes
      • Heart failure with preserved ejection fraction

    Transfusion-associated pulmonary edema

    • in patients with transfusion-associated pulmonary edema
      • if acute transfusion-associated reaction occurs
        • halt or restrict transfusion
        • manage symptoms with supportive care and monitoring
          • supportive care includes supplemental oxygen, elevation of head of bed, and prescription of diuretics and nitrates as patient's hemodynamics allow
          • monitoring includes continuous pulse oximetry, cardiac telemetry, and strict monitoring of urine output following diuretics
          • mechanical ventilation is required in most cases in accordance with clinical presentation, and has been reported to have been used in 70%-90% of patients with transfusion-related acute lung injury (TRALI)
          • renal replacement therapy may be necessary in patients with severe renal impairment
      • once initial reaction stabilized
        • evaluate and manage based on clinical presentation
          • determination of etiology of pulmonary edema essential for establishing respiratory and diuretic management
            • if TRALI or possible TRALI, consider low tidal volume strategy via invasive mechanical ventilation with maintenance of normovolemia
            • if transfusion-associated circulatory overload (TACO), consider aggressive diuretic therapy and noninvasive ventilation based on cardiopulmonary status
          • if suspected sepsis, assess and manage accordingly based on age of patient (adult or child) while waiting for confirmation via Gram stain and blood transfusion bag cultures and patient blood cultures
          • if suspected anaphylaxis, assess and manage accordingly
          • if suspected allergic reaction, look for urticaria and erythema of face and trunk, and manage accordingly
          • if chest x-ray shows presence of pulmonary edema, suspect TRALI and/or TACO (both can occur simultaneously)
          • evidence of new myocardial infarction or acute reduction in ejection fraction may suggest that pulmonary edema is not transfusion related
        • further administration of blood products and other fluids should be discontinued or limited as much as possible
    • see Transfusion-associated lung disorders for additional information

    Acute noncardiogenic pulmonary edema

    Acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS)

    • management of patients with acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS)
      • treatment is generally supportive in combination with aggressive management of precipitating conditions
      • mechanical ventilation
        • mechanical ventilation can contribute to lung injury so goal should be to maintain adequate rather than "normal" gas exchange to minimize lung injury
        • optimal ventilator settings may require heavy sedation and even neuromuscular blockade if sedation and analgesics are not successful to ensure ventilation synchrony
        • standard ARDSNet protocol for mechanical ventilation for patients with ARDS
          • set initial tidal volume to 8 mL/kg predicted body weight and reduce tidal volume by 1 mL/kg at ≤ 2-hour intervals until tidal volume is 6 mL/kg predicted body weight
          • set initial respiratory rate to approximate baseline minute ventilation (≤ 35 breaths per minute)
          • adjust tidal volume (with goal ≤ 6 mL/kg predicted body weight) and respiratory rate to achieve both
            • plateau pressure goal of ≤ 30 cm H2O
            • pH goal of 7.3-7.45
          • oxygenation goal is PaO2 55-80 mm Hg (SpO2 88%-95%)
          • consider using incremental fraction of inspired oxygen (FiO2)/positive end expiratory pressure (PEEP) combinations to achieve oxygenation goal
        • American Thoracic Society/European Society of Intensive Care Medicine/Society of Critical Care Medicine (ATS/ESICM/SCCM) 2017 recommendations on mechanical ventilation in adults with acute respiratory distress syndrome
          • limit tidal volume (4-8 mL/kg predicted body weight) and inspiratory pressure (plateau pressure < 30 cm H2O) (ATS/ESICM/SCCM Strong recommendation, Moderate confidence)
          • avoid routine use of high-frequency oscillatory ventilation in patients with moderate or severe ARDS (ATS/ESICM/SCCM Strong recommendation, High confidence)
          • consider higher rather than lower levels of positive end-expiratory pressure in patients with moderate or severe ARDS (ATS/ESICM/SCCM Conditional recommendation, Moderate confidence)
          • use prone positioning for > 12 hours per day in patients with severe ARDS (ATS/ESICM/SCCM Strong recommendation, Moderate-High confidence)
          • consider recruitment maneuvers (ATS/ESICM/SCCM Conditional recommendation, Low-Moderate confidence)
          • Reference - Am J Respir Crit Care Med 2017 May 1;195(9):1253
      • general supportive measures
        • conservative fluid management in patients with acute lung injury (ALI) improves fluid balance, reduces need for mechanical ventilation and shortens intensive care unit (ICU) stay (level 1 [likely reliable] evidence)
        • pulmonary artery catheters do not reduce mortality or length of stay for adult patients in ICU (level 1 [likely reliable] evidence)
        • venous thromboembolism prophylaxis suggested with low-molecular-weight heparin or low-dose unfractionated heparin (ACCP Grade 2C), or mechanical thromboprophylaxis if heparin contraindicated (ACCP Grade 2C)
      • referral for extracorporeal membrane oxygenation (ECMO) associated with improved survival compared to conventional ventilation support in patients with severe respiratory failure (level 2 [mid-level] evidence)
      • pharmacologic therapy
        • cisatracurium besylate (Nimbex) improves mortality in patients with severe ARDS (level 1 [likely reliable] evidence)
        • corticosteroids have conflicting evidence regarding effect on mortality in adults with ARDS
          • corticosteroids associated with
            • no significant difference in mortality in patients with unselected ARDS or sepsis-related ARDS
            • increased risk of mortality in patients with influenza related ARDS
            • decreased risk of mortality in patients with postoperative ARDS
          • consider in early moderate to severe ARDS with partial pressure of oxygen in arterial blood (PaO2) to fraction of inspired oxygen (FiO2) of < 200 and within 14 days (SCCM/ESICM Conditional recommendation, Moderate quality of evidence)
        • inhaled nitric oxide does not appear to reduce mortality and may increase risk of renal impairment (level 2 [mid-level] evidence)
        • no evidence to support use of aerosolized prostacyclin for patients with ARDS
        • surfactant
          • exogenous surfactant (calfactant) in mechanical ventilation does not reduce mortality in adults with acute respiratory distress syndrome (level 1 [likely reliable] evidence)
          • inconsistent evidence for effect of exogenous surfactant (calfactant) on mortality in children
      • see Acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS) for additional information

    Negative-pressure pulmonary edema

    • patients with negative-pressure pulmonary edema(3)
      • typically managed with endotracheal intubation and positive pressure ventilation
        • if prolonged biting or obstruction of tube, consider sedation and/or paralysis
        • if endotracheal intubation not possible (such as in patients with acute epiglottitis), consider airway access via cricothyrotomy or tracheotomy
      • other treatments that may be considered (unless specifically contraindicated)
        • diuretic therapy
        • low tidal volume ventilation
        • beta agonists
      • if severe, refractory hypoxemia, consider neuromuscular blockade, proning, or extracorporal membrane oxygenation

    High-altitude pulmonary edema (HAPE)

    • management of patients with suspected HAPE
      • descent from altitude
        • if possible, descend until symptoms resolve (WMS Grade 1A)
        • descending to 300-1,000 meters generally adequate but varies by individual
        • individuals should not descend alone
      • if descent not possible, consider
        • supplemental oxygen (WMS Grade 1B for HAPE)
        • portable hyperbaric chamber (WMS Grade 1B)
      • nifedipine sustained-release 30 mg orally 2 times daily (without a loading dose) as adjunct to descent or oxygen (WMS Grade 1C)
      • insufficient evidence to evaluate efficacy of pharmacological and nonpharmacological treatments for altitude illnesses
        • based on Cochrane review
        • Reference - Cochrane Database Syst Rev 2018 Jun 30;6:CD009567
      • see Acute altitude illnesses for additional information

    Neurogenic pulmonary edema

    • management of patients with neurogenic pulmonary edema
      • goals of management include supportive treatment for pulmonary edema with simultaneous management of underlying neurologic cause
      • Neurocritical Care Society (NCS) recommends judicious use of diuretics to achieve euvolemia and avoidance of excessive fluid intake in patients with aneurysmal subarachnoid hemorrhage and pulmonary edema or other evidence of lung injury (NCS Strong recommendation, Moderate quality evidence)
      • supportive treatment typically includes combination of volume management and mechanical ventilation
        • volume management
          • can be achieved with vasoactive drugs, diuretics, and/or fluid supplements
          • appropriate volume management can be challenging due to need to balance fluid supplementation for brain resuscitation (perfusion) with volume reduction to control pulmonary edema
        • goal of mechanical ventilation is combination of lung protective measures and measures to prevent hypoxemia and hypercapnia
          • use of low tidal volume ventilation for lung injury needs to be weighed against need for normocapnia to resolve brain injury
          • hyperventilation with partial pressure of carbon dioxide (PaCO2) goal of 32-34 mm Hg can be considered as short-term intervention to reduce intracranial pressure
          • positive end-expiratory pressure (PEEP) can be considered to improve oxygenation, but effect of PEEP on brain uncertain (may also increase intracranial pressure and decrease cerebral perfusion pressure)
      • treatment options reported to reduce intracranial pressure in patients with neurogenic pulmonary edema due to varying causes include
        • decompression and clot evacuation
        • osmotic diuretics
        • antiepileptics
        • tumor resection
        • corticosteroids
      • corticosteroids
        • may be considered in certain neurologic conditions, such as brain tumors and multiple sclerosis
        • may be contraindicated if use would exacerbate underlying neurologic condition (such as in patients with traumatic brain injury)
      • therapies reported to resolve neurogenic pulmonary edema in case reports
        • extracorporeal membrane oxygenation reported to resolve fulminant neurogenic pulmonary edema in case report of 28-year-old woman with ruptured intracranial aneurysm ( J Korean Med Sci 2013 Jun;28(6):962 full-text)
        • phentolamine IV reported to resolve neurogenic pulmonary edema in case report of 59-year-old man with ruptured arteriovenous malformation ( Chest 2012 Mar;141(3):793, commentary can be found in Chest 2012 Sep;142(3):809 )
    • see Neurogenic pulmonary edema for additional information

    Chronic pulmonary edema

    Heart failure with reduced ejection fraction

    • management of patients with heart failure with reduced ejection fraction
      • recommended guideline-directed medical therapy for all patients with symptomatic heart failure with reduced ejection fraction
        • angiotensin-converting enzyme (ACE) inhibitors (or angiotensin receptor blockers [ARBs] if intolerant to ACE inhibitors) (ACCF/AHA Class I, Level A)
          • ACE inhibitors appear to reduce mortality and rates of myocardial infarction and hospital admission in patients with left ventricular dysfunction or symptomatic heart failure (level 2 [mid-level] evidence)
          • ARBs reduce heart failure hospitalization (level 1 [likely reliable] evidence) and possibly mortality (level 2 [mid-level] evidence)
          • ARBs may have similar clinical outcomes compared with ACE inhibitors, but may be better tolerated (level 2 [mid-level] evidence)
        • beta blockers (ACCF/AHA Class I, Level A)
          • beta blockers (bisoprolol, carvedilol, or sustained-release metoprolol succinate) reduce mortality in stable patients with class II and III heart failure (level 1 [likely reliable] evidence) and possibly class IV heart failure (level 2 [mid-level] evidence)
      • recommended guideline-directed medical therapy for selected patients with symptomatic heart failure with reduced ejection fraction
        • loop diuretics for all volume overload, NYHA class II-IV patients (ACCF/AHA Class I, Level C)
          • diuretics may reduce risk of death and worsening heart failure and improve exercise capacity (level 2 [mid-level] evidence)
        • hydralazine plus isosorbide dinitrate for persistently symptomatic, NYHA class III-IV, self-identified black patients (ACCF/AHA Class I, Level A)
          • addition of isosorbide dinitrate plus hydralazine (BiDil) to standard heart failure therapy reduces mortality in black patients with advanced heart failure (level 1 [likely reliable] evidence)
        • aldosterone antagonist for NYHA class II-IV patients with serum creatinine < 2.5 mg/dL (221 mcmol/L) in men or < 2 mg/dL (177 mcmol/L) in women and potassium < 5 mEq/dL (5 mmol/L) (ACCF/AHA Class I, Level A)
          • monitor for hyperkalemia and impaired renal function
          • aldosterone blockers (eplerenone [Inspra] or spironolactone [generic, Aldactone]) reduce all-cause mortality and hospitalizations in patients with left ventricular dysfunction and heart failure or postmyocardial infarction (level 1 [likely reliable] evidence), including patients with mild symptoms (level 1 [likely reliable] evidence)
        • ivabradine
          • consider in patients in sinus rhythm, ejection fraction ≤ 35%, persisting heart rate ≥ 70 beats/minute, and persisting symptoms (New York Heart Association [NYHA] class II-IV) despite treatment with standard therapy (ESC Class IIa, Level B) (ACC/AHA/HFSA Class IIa, Level B-R)
          • in patients with left ventricular ejection fraction ≤ 35% and heart rate ≥ 70 beats/minute, ivabradine reduces hospital admission rates for worsening heart failure (level 1 [likely reliable] evidence), but may not reduce all-cause or cardiovascular mortality
          • ivabradine may reduce risk of recurrent hospitalizations for worsening heart failure (level 2 [mid-level] evidence)
      • sacubitril/valsartan
        • consider switching from ACE inhibitor or ARB to sacubitril/valsartan in patients with symptomatic heart failure, ejection fraction ≤ 35%, and elevated B-type natriuretic peptide (BNP) or N-terminal pro-BNP levels, but be aware of risk for symptomatic hypotension
        • replacement of angiotensin-converting enzyme (ACE) inhibitors or angiotensin receptor blockers (ARB) with angiotensin receptor–neprilysin inhibitor (ARNI) is recommended to further reduce morbidity and mortality for patients with chronic symptomatic (New York Heart Association class II or III) heart failure with reduced ejection fraction (ACC/AHA/HFSA Class I, Level B-R)
        • sacubitril/valsartan recommended instead of ACE inhibitor or angiotensin receptor blocker (unless contraindicated) in patients with New York Heart Association class II-III heart failure and left ventricular ejection fraction ≤ 40% with ongoing symptoms of heart failure despite optimal treatment (SIGN Strong recommendation)
        • sacubitril/valsartan recommended as replacement for ACE inhibitor to further reduce risk of death and heart failure hospitalization in ambulatory patients with heart failure with reduced ejection fraction and each of the following (ESC Class I, Level B)
          • symptomatic despite optimal treatment with an ACE inhibitor, beta blocker, and a mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist
          • plasma BNP ≥ 150 pg/mL or plasma NT-proBNP ≥ 600 pg/mL, or if heart failure hospitalization within last 12 months, plasma BNP ≥ 100 pg/mL or plasma NT-proBNP ≥ 400 pg/mL
          • able to tolerate enalapril 10 mg orally twice daily
        • treatment with sacubitril/valsartan, beta blocker, and mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist reduces risk of sudden death and is recommended for patients with heart failure with reduced ejection fraction and ventricular arrhythmias (ESC Class I, Level A)
        • sacubitril/valsartan may decrease mortality, heart failure hospitalization, and risk for worsening heart failure compared to enalapril in patients with symptomatic heart failure and ejection fraction ≤ 35%, but may increase risk for symptomatic hypotension (level 2 [mid-level] evidence)
        • review of new class of drugs for systolic heart failure: the PARADIGM-HF study can be found in Cleve Clin J Med 2015 Oct;82(10):693
      • other medications that may be used in selected patients with heart failure include digoxin, trimetazidine, anticoagulants, statins, IV iron supplementation, amiodarone, n-3 polyunsaturated fatty acids, and coenzyme Q10
      • measures to reduce hospitalization in patients at high risk of rehospitalization include
        • heart failure structured management and education
          • comprehensive discharge planning with post discharge support reduces readmission rates in heart failure patients (level 1 [likely reliable] evidence)
          • interventions which may reduce mortality and hospitalizations include (level 2 [mid-level] evidence)
            • multidisciplinary disease management programs
            • N-terminal pro-B-type natriuretic peptide-guided intensive patient management
            • telephonic case management or support
          • multidisciplinary heart failure disease-management programs recommended to facilitate implementation of guideline-directed medical therapy and to address different barriers to behavioral change (ACCF/AHA Class I, Level B)
        • exercise training may be safe in patients with heart failure and may reduce heart failure-related hospitalizations in patients with systolic heart failure (level 2 [mid-level] evidence) (ACCF/AHA Class I, Level A)
        • wireless pulmonary artery pressure monitoring reduces heart failure-related hospitalizations in patients with NYHA Class III heart failure and previous heart failure hospitalization (level 1 [likely reliable] evidence)
      • therapies that may be used in patients with medication-refractory heart failure or under specific circumstances include
        • implanted cardiac devices
          • implantable cardioverter-defibrillators (ICDs) may reduce risk of death in specific patient populations
          • cardiac resynchronization therapy can be used in selected patients with medication refractory heart failure to improve cardiac function and treatment of heart failure
          • mechanical circulatory support may improve cardiac output in patients with heart failure refractory to medical management
        • cardiac transplantation - improvement in survival appears limited to patients with worst prognosis prior to surgery (level 2 [mid-level] evidence)
      • sodium restriction not well supported by evidence, but considered reasonable for patients with symptomatic heart failure to reduce congestive symptoms (ACCF/AHA Class IIa, Level C)
      • long-term follow-up should include monitoring of
        • serum electrolytes and renal function (ACCF/AHA Class I, Level C)
        • volume status and supine/upright hypotension (ACCF/AHA Class I, Level B)
        • measurement of ejection fraction and measurement of severity of structural remodeling in patients with (ACCF/AHA Class I, Level C)
          • significant change in clinical status
          • clinical event (or recovery from clinical event)
          • treatment that may affect cardiac function
          • potential candidacy for device therapy
    • see Heart failure with reduced ejection fraction for additional information

    Heart failure with preserved ejection fraction

    • management of patients with heart failure with preserved ejection fraction
      • initial management of patients with acute heart failure and preserved ejection fraction is tailored toward treatment of volume overload, while maintaining adequate filling pressures (so as not to over-diurese), and heart rate control
      • see Acute heart failure topic for additional information on emergency treatment
      • patient education regarding diet, medications, activity and exercise, symptoms of worsening heart failure (seek medical attention if rapid weight gain in short period of time)
      • diuretics recommended for relief of symptoms due to volume overload (ACCF/AHA Class I, Level C; ACC/AHA Class I, Level C-EO; ESC Class I, Level B)
      • control systolic and diastolic hypertension (ACCF/AHA Class I, Level B)
        • beta blockers, ACE inhibitors, and angiotensin receptor blockers (ARBs) are reasonable choices for patients with hypertension and heart failure with preserved ejection fraction (ACCF/AHA Class IIa, Level C)
      • perindopril might reduce unplanned heart failure related hospitalization rates in patients with diastolic dysfunction (level 2 [mid-level] evidence)
      • angiotensin receptor blockers (ARBs) might reduce hospitalization for heart failure but not total hospitalizations or mortality in patients with symptomatic heart failure and ejection fraction > 40% (level 2 [mid-level] evidence)
      • consider aldosterone receptor antagonists to decrease hospitalizations in patients with ejection fraction ≥ 45%, elevated brain natriuretic peptide levels or heart failure admission within 1 year, estimated glomerular filtration rate > 30 mL/minute, serum creatinine < 2.5 mg/dL, and potassium < 5 mEq/L (ACC/AHA/HFSA Class IIb, Level B-NR)
      • beta blockers may reduce mortality but may not affect heart failure hospitalization in patients with heart failure with preserved ejection fraction (level 2 [mid-level] evidence)
      • wireless pulmonary artery pressure monitoring reduces heart failure-related hospitalizations in patients with New York Heart Association (NYHA) Class III heart failure and previous heart failure hospitalization (level 1 [likely reliable] evidence)
      • coronary revascularization reasonable in patients with coronary artery disease in whom symptomatic or demonstrable myocardial ischemia is judged to have adverse effect on heart failure symptoms (ACCF/AHA Class IIa, Level C)
      • additional evidence regarding drugs for heart failure with preserved ejection fraction is limited
        • spironolactone may decrease risk of hospitalization for heart failure (but not cardiovascular mortality) in patients with heart failure with preserved ejection fraction (level 2 [mid-level] evidence)
        • digoxin does not reduce morbidity or mortality in patients with heart failure with preserved ejection fraction and normal sinus rhythm (level 1 [likely reliable] evidence)
        • sildenafil does not improve cardiovascular endurance (level 1 [likely reliable] evidence)
        • isosorbide mononitrate titrated up to 120 mg orally daily over 2 weeks does not increase daily activity or improve quality of life in patients with heart failure with preserved ejection fraction (level 1 [likely reliable] evidence)
    • see Heart failure with preserved ejection fraction for additional information

    Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy

    • management of patients with hypertrophic cardiomyopathy
      • for asymptomatic patients, treat comorbidities that might contribute to cardiovascular disease (ACCF/AHA Class I, Level C)
      • activity
        • low intensity aerobic exercise suggested as a component of a healthy lifestyle for patients with asymptomatic hypertrophic cardiomyopathy (ACCF/AHA Class IIa, Level C)
        • participation in intense competitive sports is not recommended for patients with hypertrophic cardiomyopathy (ACCF/AHA Class III, Level C; ESC Class I, Level C )
        • participation reasonable for (ACCF/AHA Class IIa, Level C)
          • low intensity competitive sports such as golf and bowling
          • a range of recreational sporting activities such swimming laps, modest hiking, and skating
      • medications for symptom treatment of obstructive or nonobstructive disease
        • beta blockers recommended to treat angina or dyspnea in adults, with caution in patients with sinus bradycardia or severe conduction disease (ACCF/AHA Class I, Level B)
        • in patients who do not respond to low doses of beta blockers for angina or dyspnea, titrate dose to resting heart rate < 60-65 bpm and up to the generally accepted recommended maximum dose of the specific drug (ACCF/AHA Class I, Level B)
        • in patients who do not respond to beta blockers, have side effects, or contraindications, verapamil recommended to treat angina or dyspnea, (ACCF/AHA Class I, Level B)
          • titrate from low doses up to 480 mg daily
          • use caution in patients with high outflow gradients, heart failure, sinus bradycardia
        • diltiazem may be considered for patients who do not tolerate verapamil or in whom verapamil is contraindicated (ACCF/AHA Class IIb, Level C)
        • in children or adolescents, consider beta blockers to treat angina or dyspnea but monitor for side effects (ACCF/AHA Class IIb, Level C)
        • left ventricular apical aneurysms rarely require treatment, but if thrombi present within aneurysm, treatment usually includes long-term anticoagulation
      • implantable cardioverter defibrillators (ICDs)
        • recommended for patients with a history of any of the following if meaningful survival > 1 year expected
          • cardiac arrest (ACCF/AHA Class I, Level B; ESC Class I, Level B ) due to ventricular tachycardia or ventricular fibrillation (AHA/ACC/HRS Class I, Level B-NR)
          • ventricular fibrillation (ACCF/AHA Class I, Level B; ESC Class I, Level B )
          • hemodynamically significant ventricular tachycardia (ACCF/AHA Class I, Level B; ESC Class I, Level B )
          • spontaneous sustained ventricular tachycardia causing syncope or hemodynamic compromise (AHA/ACC/HRS Class I, Level B-NR)
        • implantable cardioverter-defibrillators reported to terminate ventricular arrhythmias in patients with hypertrophic cardiomyopathy and high risk of sudden death (level 3 [lacking direct] evidence)
    • see Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy for additional information

    Valvular disease

    Mitral stenosis

    • management of patients with mitral stenosis
      • heart rate control
        • in mitral stenosis patients with atrial fibrillation, heart rate control may be beneficial if fast ventricular response (AHA/ACC Class IIa, Level C)
        • in mitral stenosis patients in normal sinus rhythm, consider beta blocker if symptoms during exercise (AHA/ACC Class IIb, Level B)
      • in asymptomatic severe stenosis (stage C)
        • percutaneous balloon valvuloplasty may be indicated for patients with
          • very severe stenosis (mitral valve area [MVA] ≤ 1 cm2) and favorable valve morphology without left atrial thrombus or severe mitral regurgitation (AHA/ACC Class IIa, Level C)
          • new onset atrial fibrillation who have favorable valve morphology without left atrial thrombus or severe mitral regurgitation (AHA/ACC Class IIb, Level C)
          • unfavorable characteristics and high risk of thromboembolism or hemodynamic compensation (ESC/EACTS Level IIa, Level C)
        • mitral valve surgery indicated if patient undergoing another cardiac surgery (AHA/ACC Class I, Level C)
      • in symptomatic severe stenosis (stage D)
        • percutaneous balloon valvuloplasty
          • indicated for patients with favorable valve morphology without left atrial thrombus or moderate-to-severe mitral regurgitation (AHA/ACC Class I, Level A; ESC/EACTS Class I, Level B)
          • may be indicated for patients with severe symptoms (New York Heart Association [NYHA] class III-IV) with severe mitral stenosis (MVA ≤ 1.5 cm2, stage D) who have a suboptimal valve anatomy and who are not candidates for or at high risk for surgery (AHA/ACC Class IIb, Level C; ESC/EACTS Class I, Level C)
        • mitral valve surgery indicated for patients with
          • another indication for cardiac surgery (AHA/ACC Class I, Level C)
          • NYHA Class III-IV who are not high risk for surgery and not candidate for or have failed prior balloon commissurotomy (AHA/ACC Class I, Level B)
        • mitral valve surgery may also be indicated for patients with recurrent embolic events while receiving adequate anticoagulation, with surgery to include excision of the left atrial appendage (AHA/ACC Class IIb, Level C)
    • see Mitral Stenosis for additional information

    Mitral regurgitation

    • management of patients with mitral regurgitation
      • mitral valve surgery for mitral regurgitation is defined as either mitral valve replacement or repair
      • patients with acute severe mitral regurgitation
        • urgent surgery often indicated after stabilization of hemodynamic status
        • usual intervention is valve replacement
        • medications such as positive inotropes, vasodilators, and diuretics may be used acutely for symptom control
        • mechanical support (for example, with intra-aortic balloon pump) may be used prior to or during valve replacement
      • patients with chronic primary (degenerative) mitral regurgitation
        • medical therapy
          • medical therapy may be considered if symptomatic (stage D) and left ventricular ejection fraction < 60% if surgery not contemplated
          • vasodilator therapy not indicated if normotensive and asymptomatic (stages B and C1) with normal left ventricular systolic function
        • mitral valve surgery
          • recommended in the following patients
            • asymptomatic (stage C2) mitral regurgitation and left ventricular ejection fraction ≤ 60% and/or left ventricular end-systolic diameter (LVESD) ≥ 40 mm (AHA/ACC Class I, Level B), or LVESD ≥ 45 mm (ESC/EACTS Class I, Level C)
            • severe mitral regurgitation if undergoing cardiac surgery for other indications (AHA/ACC Class I, Level B)
            • symptomatic (stage D) mitral regurgitation and left ventricular ejection fraction > 30% (AHA/ACC Class I, Level B), with left ventricular end-systolic diameter < 55 mm (ESC/EACTS Class I, Level B)
          • may be considered in the following patients
            • asymptomatic (stage C1) mitral regurgitation and preserved left ventricular (LV) function (LV ejection fraction > 60% and LVSED < 40 mm) with progressive increase in left ventricular size or decrease in ejection fraction on serial imaging (AHA/ACC Class IIa, Level C-LD)
            • asymptomatic mitral regurgitation with preserved left ventricular function and either (ESC/EACTS Class IIa, Level C)
              • new onset atrial fibrillation
              • resting pulmonary hypertension (systolic pulmonary pressure > 50 mm Hg)
            • asymptomatic mitral regurgitation with preserved left ventricular function if all of (ESC/EACTS Class IIa, Level C)
              • high likelihood of durable repair
              • flail leaflet
              • low surgical risk
              • left ventricular end-systolic diameter ≥ 40 mm
            • asymptomatic mitral regurgitation with preserved left-ventricular function, low surgical risk, high likelihood of durable repair, and either (ESC/EACTS Class IIb, Level C)
              • pulmonary hypertension on exercise (systolic pulmonary artery pressure ≥ 60 mm Hg during exercise)
              • left atrial dilation (volume index ≥ 60 mL/m2 body surface area [BSA]) and sinus rhythm
            • severe left ventricular dysfunction present defined as left ventricular ejection fraction < 30% and/or left ventricular end-systolic diameter > 55 mm and both (ESC/EACTS Class IIa, Level C)
              • refractory to medical therapy
              • high likelihood of durable repair and low comorbidity
            • severe left ventricular dysfunction present defined as left ventricular ejection fraction < 30% and/or left ventricular end-systolic diameter > 55 mm and both (ESC/EACTS Class IIb, Level C)
              • refractory to medical therapy
              • low likelihood of durable repair and low comorbidity
            • symptomatic severe (stage D) mitral regurgitation and left ventricular ejection fraction ≤ 30% (AHA/ACC Class IIb, Level C)
        • mitral valve repair is recommended
          • as preferred technique when result of repair expected to be durable in patients with severe primary (degenerative) mitral regurgitation (ESC/EACTS Class I, Level C)
          • over mitral valve replacement in patients with chronic severe primary (degenerative) mitral regurgitation requiring surgical treatment to either (AHA/ACC Class I, Level B)
            • posterior leaflet alone
            • anterior or both leaflets when result of repair expected to be successful and durable
          • may be considered in the following patients
            • asymptomatic severe (stage C1) mitral regurgitation and left ventricular ejection fraction > 60% and left ventricular end-systolic diameter < 40 mm if either
              • nonrheumatic disease present and repair likely to be successful and durable in patients with either of the following (AHA/ACC Class IIa, Level B)
                • new-onset atrial fibrillation
                • resting pulmonary hypertension (pulmonary artery systolic arterial pressure > 50 mm Hg)
              • both of the following are present (AHA/ACC Class IIa, Level B)
                • > 95% chance that repair likely to be successful and durable without residual mitral regurgitation
                • expected mortality < 1% when performed at Heart Valve Center of Excellence
            • asymptomatic moderate (stage B) mitral regurgitation when having cardiac surgery for other indications (AHA/ACC Class IIa, Level C)
        • transcatheter mitral valve repair may be considered for severely symptomatic (New York Heart Association class III to IV) patients with severe (stage D) mitral regurgitation with favorable anatomy for repair procedure and both (AHA/ACC Class IIb, Level B)
          • reasonable life expectancy
          • prohibitive surgical risk due to severe comorbid conditions with symptoms despite optimal guideline-directed medical therapy for heart failure
        • mitral valve replacement should not be performed for treatment of isolated severe mitral regurgitation limited to < 50% of posterior leaflet unless mitral valve repair attempted and was unsuccessful (AHA/ACC Class III, Level B)
      • patients with chronic secondary (functional) mitral regurgitation
        • guideline-directed medical therapy for heart failure including ACE inhibitors, angiotensin-receptor blockers, beta blockers, and/or aldosterone antagonists recommended for stages B to D mitral regurgitation if heart failure and reduced left ventricular ejection fraction present (AHA/ACC Class I, Level A)
        • cardiac resynchronization therapy with biventricular pacing recommended for patients with symptomatic chronic severe secondary mitral regurgitation (stages B to D) who meet indications for device therapy use
        • mitral valve surgery
          • recommended if severe mitral regurgitation (defined as effective regurgitant orifice area ≥ 20 mm2 and regurgitant volume > 30 mL) and left ventricular ejection fraction > 30% if having coronary artery bypass graft procedure (ESC/EACTS Class I, Level C)
          • may be considered in the following patients
            • moderate mitral regurgitation if having coronary artery bypass graft procedure (ESC/EACTS Class IIa, Level C)
            • severe (stages C and D) mitral regurgitation if having coronary artery bypass graft or aortic valve replacement procedure (AHA/ACC Class IIa, Level C)
            • symptomatic severe mitral regurgitation and left ventricular ejection fraction < 30%, if both (ESC/EACTS Class IIa, Level C)
              • option for revascularization
              • evidence of viability
            • severely symptomatic (New York Heart Association class III to IV) with severe (stage D) mitral regurgitation and persistent symptoms despite optimal guideline-directed medical therapy for heart failure (AHA/ACC Class IIb, Level B)
            • severe mitral regurgitation and left ventricular ejection fraction > 30% if all of (ESC/EACTS Class IIb, Level C)
              • symptomatic despite optimal medical management (including cardiac resynchronization if indicated)
              • revascularization not indicated
              • low comorbidities
        • mitral valve repair may be considered in patients with moderate mitral regurgitation (stage B) if having other cardiac surgery (AHA/ACC Class IIb, Level C)
    • see Mitral Regurgitation for additional information

    Aortic stenosis

    • management of patients with aortic stenosis
      • in asymptomatic severe aortic stenosis (stage C)
        • no medical therapy proven to reduce progression, but echocardiography monitoring suggested (AHA/ACC Class I, Level C)
        • aortic valve replacement (AVR) may be indicated for
          • left ventricular ejection fraction (LVEF) < 50% (stage C2) (AHA/ACC Class I, Level B; ESC/EACTS Class I, Level C)
          • having cardiac surgery for other indications (stage C or D) (AHA/ACC Class I, Level B; ESC/EACTS Class I, Level C)
          • asymptomatic patients with very severe aortic stenosis (stage C1) (AHA/ACC Class IIa, Level B; ESC/EACTS Class IIa, Level C)
          • apparently asymptomatic patients with severe aortic stenosis (stage C1) and decreased exercise tolerance or fall in systolic blood pressure on exercise testing (AHA/ACC Class IIa, Level B; ESC/EACTS Class IIa, Level C)
      • in symptomatic severe aortic stenosis (stage D)
        • AVR indicated for patients with severe aortic stenosis if symptoms (stage D1) (AHA/ACC Class I, Level B; ESC/EACTS Class I, Level B)
        • AVR may be considered for symptomatic patients with low-flow/low-gradient severe aortic stenosis if either
          • reduced LVEF and severe aortic stenosis on low-dose dobutamine infusion (stage D2) (AHA/ACC Class IIa, Level B; ESC/EACTS Class IIa, Level C)
          • normal LVEF (stage D3) but with evidence that valve obstruction is most likely cause of symptoms (AHA/ACC Class IIa, Level C; ESC/EACTS Class IIa, Level C)
        • percutaneous aortic balloon valvuloplasty may be considered in patients with symptomatic severe aortic stenosis as a bridge to AVR (AHA/ACC Class IIb, Level C; ESC/EACTS Class IIb, Level C) or as a palliative measure
      • AVR may improve survival in asymptomatic and symptomatic patients with severe aortic stenosis (level 2 [mid-level] evidence)
        • AVR may be surgical AVR [SAVR] or transcatheter aortic valve implantation (TAVI) (also called transcatheter aortic valve replacement [TAVR])
          • guidelines vary on the use of surgical AVR versus TAVI for patients with low to intermediate surgical risk:
            • American Heart Association for surgical AVR versus TAVR
              • surgical AVR recommended when AVR indicated if low or intermediate surgical risk (AHA/ACC Class I, Level B-NR )
              • surgical AVR or TAVR recommended for symptomatic patients with severe aortic stenosis (stage D) and high risk for surgical AVR (AHA/ACC Class I, Level A)
            • BMJ Rapid Recommendations for TAVI versus SAVR for patients with severe symptomatic aortic stenosis at low to intermediate perioperative risk
              • transfemoral TAVI recommended rather than SAVR in patients aged 85 and older eligible for TAVI or SAVR (GRADE Strong recommendation)
              • transfemoral TAVI suggested rather than SAVR in patients aged 75 to < 85 eligible for TAVI or SAVR (GRADE Weak recommendation)
              • SAVR suggested rather than transfemoral TAVI in patients aged 65 to < 75 eligible for TAVI or SAVR (GRADE Weak recommendation)
              • SAVR recommended rather than transfemoral TAVI in patients < 65 years old and eligible for TAVI or SAVR (GRADE Strong recommendation)
              • Reference - BMJ 2016 Sep 28;354:i5085; full text, full details at MAGICapp 2016 Oct 1
          • TAVI recommended in patients with prohibitive surgical risk and predicted post-TAVR survival > 12 months (AHA/ACC Class I, Level B; ESC/EACTS Class I, Level B)
          • transfemoral TAVI may reduce 2-year mortality compared to surgical aortic valve replacement in patients with severe aortic stenosis at low or intermediate risk of perioperative death (level 2 [mid-level] evidence)
          • in patients with intermediate-risk severe aortic stenosis, TAVI and surgical AVR associated with similar risk of death or disabling stroke at 2 years, but TAVR associated with lower risk of serious bleeding and new atrial fibrillation (level 2 [mid-level] evidence)
          • TAVI with SAPIEN 3 valve may decrease risk of death and stroke compared to surgical aortic valve replacement in patients with intermediate-risk severe aortic stenosis (level 2 [mid-level] evidence)
          • in patients with severe aortic stenosis and heart failure symptoms, TAVI with self-expanding bioprosthesis appears at least effective as surgical AVR for 1-year mortality (level 2 [mid-level] evidence)
          • in patients not suitable for conventional surgery TAVI may reduce 2-year mortality, rehospitalization, and cardiac symptoms but increase risk of stroke, major bleeding, and other vascular complications (level 2 [mid-level] evidence)
        • in patients having surgical AVR
          • mechanical valve appears to have lower reoperation rate than bioprosthetic valve for aortic valve replacement (level 2 [mid-level] evidence), but associated with greater need for anticoagulation
            • bioprosthesis recommended if anticoagulant therapy is contraindicated or not desired (AHA/ACC Class I, Level C; ESC/EACTS Class I, Level C) and reasonable in patients > 65-70 years old (AHA/ACC Class IIa, Level B; ESC/EACTS Class IIa, Level C)
            • mechanical prosthesis reasonable for patients < 60 years old without contraindication to anticoagulation (AHA/ACC Class IIa, Level B; ESC/EACTS Class IIa, Level C)
            • mechanical valve or bioprosthesis reasonable in patients aged 60-70 years (AHA/ACC Class IIa, Level B)
          • limited sternotomy and standard median sternotomy for aortic valve replacement associated with similar in-hospital and 30-day mortality, but limited sternotomy may reduce duration of intensive care and hospitalization (level 2 [mid-level] evidence)
      • medical therapy has limited direct role in treatment of aortic stenosis
        • hypertension may be treated with low starting doses, gradual titration, and frequent clinical monitoring (AHA/ACC Class I, Level B)
        • vasodilators may be used with invasive hemodynamic monitoring for decompensated heart failure (AHA/ACC Class IIb, Level C)
        • statins do not appear to reduce mortality or need for aortic valve surgery (level 2 [mid-level] evidence) and are not associated with slower progression of aortic stenosis (level 3 [lacking direct] evidence) (AHA/ACC Class III No Benefit, Level A)
    • see Aortic Stenosis for additional information

    Aortic regurgitation

    • in asymptomatic mild to moderate aortic regurgitation (stage B), monitoring with periodic echocardiography may detect progression to severe aortic regurgitation
    • in asymptomatic severe aortic regurgitation (stage C)
      • medications do not have a clearly established role in asymptomatic aortic regurgitation
      • aortic valve replacement (AVR)
        • recommended if
          • left ventricular systolic dysfunction (ejection fraction < 50%) at rest (stage C2) when systolic dysfunction not due to another cause (AHA/ACC Class I, Level B; ESC/EACTS Class I, Level B)
          • having cardiac surgery for other reason (AHA/ACC Class I, Level C), specifically surgery of ascending aorta or another valve (ESC/EACTS Class I, Level C); AVR should also be considered if having cardiac surgery in patients with moderate aortic regurgitation (AHA/ACC Class IIa, Level C)
        • should be considered if severe left ventricular dilation, specifically either
          • left ventricular end-systolic diameter [LVESD] > 50 mm or LVESD > 25 mm/m2 body surface area (AHA/ACC Class IIa, Level B; ESC/EACTS Class IIa, Level C)
          • left ventricular end-diastolic diameter > 70 mm (ESC/EACTS Class IIa, Level C)
        • may be considered for asymptomatic patients with normal left ventricular systolic function at rest (ejection fraction ≥ 50%, stage C1) with progressive severe left ventricular dilation (left ventricular end-diastolic diameter > 65 mm) if low surgical risk (AHA/ACC Class IIb, Level C)
    • in symptomatic severe aortic regurgitation (stage D)
      • AVR recommended for patients with symptomatic severe aortic regurgitation regardless of left ventricular systolic function (stage D) (AHA/ACC Class I, Level B; ESC/EACTS Class I, Level B)
      • ACE inhibitor or angiotensin-receptor blocker (ARB), and beta blocker, may be considered if surgery contraindicated (AHA/ACC Class IIa, Level B)
      • short-term vasodilators and inotropic agents may be used to bridge patients with severe heart failure awaiting valve surgery
    • aortic valve repair may be an alternative to AVR in suitable patients with dilated annulus, cusp perforation, cusp prolapse, or cusp restriction
    • aortic root replacement
      • recommended for patients with Marfan syndrome and aortic root disease (maximal ascending aortic diameter ≥ 50 mm) (ESC/EACTS Class I, Level C)
      • recommended for asymptomatic patients with bicuspid aortic valve if aortic root or ascending aorta diameter ≥ 55 mm (AHA/ACC Class I, Level B)
      • may be considered for other patients with aortic root disease if certain criteria are met
    • transcatheter aortic valve intervention/repair (TAVI/TAVR) may be considered as off-label procedure in carefully selected patients with aortic regurgitation who are not candidates for standard aortic valve replacement
    • see Aortic Regurgitation for additional information

    Recommendation grading systems used

    • Society of Critical Care Medicine (SCCM) uses Grading of Recommendations, Assessment, Development, and Evaluation (GRADE)
      • strength of recommendation
        • Strong - benefits clearly outweigh risks and burdens (or vice versa) for most, if not all, patients
        • Conditional - benefits and risks closely balanced and/or uncertain
        • Best practice statement - recommendation not conducive to GRADE process
      • quality of evidence
        • High - randomized trials without factors that reduce quality of evidence, or well done observational studies with very large magnitude of effect
        • Moderate - downgraded randomized trials or upgraded observational studies
        • Low - well done observational studies, or randomized trials with many limitations
        • Very low - downgraded observational studies (results from individual studies at serious or very serious risk of bias, serious inconsistencies in results across studies, indirect evidence, sparse or imprecise data, or likely publication bias)
      • Reference - Society of Critical Care Medicine (SCCM) and European Society of Intensive Care Medicine (ESICM) guidelines for diagnosis and management of critical illness-related corticosteroid insufficiency (CIRCI) in critically ill patients (Part I) ( Intensive Care Med 2017 Dec;43(12):1751)
    • European Society of Intensive Care Medicine (ESICM) grading system for recommendations
      • strength of recommendation
        • Grade 1 (strong) - most people would want recommended course of action and only a small proportion would not
        • Grade 2 (conditional) - majority of people would want recommended course of action, but many would not
        • Best Practice Statement (BPS) - ungraded strong recommendation in which benefit or harm was unequivocal but evidence was difficult to categorize by GRADE methodology
      • evidence rating
        • Grade A (High) - confidence that true effect lies close to estimate of effect
        • Grade B (Moderate) - moderately confident in effect estimate; true effect likely to be close to estimate of effect, but possible it is substantially different
        • Grade C (Low) - limited confidence in effect estimate; true effect may be substantially different from estimate of effect
        • Grade D (Very low) - very little confidence in effect estimate; true effect likely to be substantially different from estimate of effect
      • Reference - ESICM guideline on prevention of acute kidney injury and protection of renal function in intensive care unit: 2017 update ( Intensive Care Med 2017 Jun;43(6):730 full-text)
    • American College of Cardiology Foundation/American Heart Association (ACCF/AHA) grading system for recommendations
      • classifications of recommendations
        • Class I - procedure or treatment should be performed or administered
        • Class IIa - reasonable to perform procedure or administer treatment, but additional studies with focused objectives needed
        • Class IIb - procedure or treatment may be considered; additional studies with broad objectives needed, additional registry data would be useful
        • Class III - procedure or treatment should not be performed or administered because it is not helpful or may be harmful
          • Class III ratings may be subclassified as Class III No Benefit or Class III Harm
      • levels of evidence
        • Level A - data derived from multiple randomized clinical trials or meta-analyses
        • Level B - data derived from single randomized trial or nonrandomized studies
        • Level C - only expert opinion, case studies, or standard of care
      • Reference - ACCF/AHA 2013 guideline on management of heart failure ( Circulation 2013 Oct 15;128(16):e240)
    • European Society of Cardiology (ESC) grading system for recommendations
    • classes of recommendations
      • Class I - evidence and/or general agreement that given treatment or procedure is beneficial, useful, and effective
      • Class II - conflicting evidence and/or divergence of opinion about usefulness/efficacy of given treatment or procedure
        • Class IIa - weight of evidence/opinion in favor of usefulness/efficacy
        • Class IIb - usefulness/efficacy less well established by evidence/opinion
      • Class III - evidence or general agreement that given treatment or procedure is not useful/effective, and in some cases may be harmful
    • levels of evidence
      • Level A - data derived from multiple randomized clinical trials or meta-analyses
      • Level B - data derived from single randomized clinical trial or large nonrandomized studies
      • Level C - consensus of opinion of experts and/or small studies, retrospective studies, registries
    • Reference - ESC guideline on diagnosis and treatment of acute and chronic heart failure ( Eur Heart J 2016 Jul 14;37(27):2129 full-text), correction can be found in Eur Heart J 2018 Mar 7;39(10):860, editorial can be found in Eur Heart J 2016 Jul 14;37(27):2096
  • Scottish Intercollegiate Guidelines Network (SIGN) grading system
    • strength of recommendation
      • Strong - intervention should or should not be used; guideline development group confident that for vast majority of people intervention will do more harm than good or vice versa
      • Conditional - intervention should be considered
        • guideline development group confident intervention will do more good than harm for most patients
        • choice of intervention more likely to vary depending on person's values and preferences so healthcare professionals should spend more time discussing options with patients
      • Good Practice Point - recommended best practice based on clinical experience of guideline development group
    • levels of evidence
      • Level 1++ - high-quality meta-analyses, systematic reviews of randomized controlled trials (RCTs) or RCTs with very low risk of bias
      • Level 1+ - well-conducted meta-analyses, systematic reviews, or RCTs with low risk of bias
      • Level 1- - meta-analyses, systematic reviews, or RCTs with high risk of bias
      • Level 2++
        • high-quality systematic reviews of case-control or cohort studies
        • high-quality case-control or cohort studies with very low risk of confounding or bias and high probability that relationship is causal
      • Level 2+ - well-conducted case-control or cohort studies with low risk of confounding or bias and moderate probability that relationship is causal
      • Level 2- - case-control or cohort studies with high risk of confounding or bias and significant risk that relationship not causal
      • Level 3 - nonanalytic studies (such as case reports, case series)
      • Level 4 - expert opinion
    • Reference - SIGN national clinical guideline on management of chronic heart failure (SIGN 2016 Mar PDF)
  • American Thoracic Society/European Society of Intensive Care Medicine/Society of Critical Care Medicine (ATS/ESICM/SCCM) grading system
  • strength of recommendations
    • Strong recommendation
      • most patients should receive the recommended course of action
      • most patients would want the intervention, only a small proportion would not
    • Conditional/Weak recommendation
      • clinicians should be more prepared to help patients make decisions consistent with patient's values
      • most patients would want the intervention, but many would not
  • quality of evidence determination and implications/confidence in estimate of effect
    • High
      • randomized trial without limitation in study quality, indirectness, important inconsistency, sparse or imprecise data, or high probability of publication bias
      • further research is very unlikely to change confidence in estimate of effect
    • Moderate
      • downgraded randomized trial or upgraded observational study with strong association (no plausible confounders), evidence of a dose-response gradient, or plausible confounders which would have reduced the effect
      • further research is likely to have important impact on confidence in estimate of effect and may change estimate
    • Low
      • well-done observational study with control groups
      • further research is very likely to have important impact on confidence in estimate of effect and is likely to change estimate
    • Very low
      • any other evidence (for example, case reports, case series)
      • very uncertain about estimate
